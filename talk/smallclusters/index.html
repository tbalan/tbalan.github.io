Non-proportional hazards or unobserved heterogeneity in clustered survival data: Can we tell the difference?

Abstract

In survival analysis, shared frailty (random effect) models are often used in the context of heterogeneous survival data, such as clustered failures or recurrent events. In [Hougaard (2000)] a large family of distributions for the random effects were proposed, including the positive stable and compound Poisson distributions with mass at 0. The estimation of semiparametric frailty models with general distributions has proved challenging. It is common to assume that the effects of the covariates are proportional on the hazard scale, although this implies, for most frailty distributions, that the marginal effect is non-proportional. For the situation when clusters are small, or there are only a few recurrent events per individual, it is difficult to distinguish between a non-proportional effect of the covariates and the presence of a random effect. We introduce a novel software implementation of the semiparametric shared frailty model with a very flexible choice for the frailty distribution, consistently implemented for clustered failures with left truncation and recurrent event data analysis. With these means, we study the small sample behaviour of the tests statistics for the presence of the random effects, the sensitivity to the proportional hazards assumption, and the coverage of confidence intervals.
